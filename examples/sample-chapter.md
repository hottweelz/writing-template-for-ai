The key the solicitor had given her was the wrong key.

Nora tried it twice anyway, because trying it a third time was the kind of thing that wasted a ferry. The lock held. Salt had got into the works and made a paste of the mechanism, and the door — her father's door, the cottage door, the door she had last walked out of at twenty-eight — stayed shut against the one daughter who had come back to sell it.

Behind her the ferry was already small. The man who ran it had not waited to see whether she got in.

"He kept a spare under the second step."

She did not turn around immediately. On the island you learned to place a voice before you placed a face, and she had not heard this one in eleven years, and she had heard it every day for the eighteen before that.

"Mrs. Aldous."

"Just Aldous now." The old woman came up the path the way the path allowed, sideways, one hand on the rail her father had bolted into the rock. "He told me you'd come on the last ferry. Not the first. He was specific about that."

That was the thing Nora would think about later, on the nights the logbooks kept her up. Not the lighthouse, gone dark for a decade and worth less than the cost of pulling it down. Not the cottage. The fact that a man she had not spoken to in eleven years had known, to the ferry, when she would finally arrive — and had told the neighbor, and not her.

She found the spare under the second step.

* * *

The lamp room smelled of cold brass and old wicks. She went up because the cottage was full of his coats and she was not ready for his coats.

The logbooks were where the keepers' logbooks always were, shelved by year. She expected weather. Tides, wind, the dry liturgy of a man whose whole job had been to notice when the dark was coming and do something about it.

She opened the last one. The columns were there, ruled and filled — and in the margin beside them, written sideways so it ran the length of the page, in handwriting she would have known blind:

*N. If you're reading this in order, you stayed. Good. I owe you the long version.*

Nora sat down on the iron stair and did not, for a while, read the next page.
