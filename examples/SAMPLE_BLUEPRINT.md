## §1. Executive Concept

- **One-sentence premise:** A grown woman returns to the decommissioned lighthouse where her father drowned to settle his estate, and finds the logbook he kept the year he stopped speaking to her.
- **One-paragraph premise:** Nora Vale has not been back to the island in eleven years. When her father dies, the solicitor's letter pulls her home to sell a lighthouse nobody wants and a cottage full of a silence she helped build. In the lamp room she finds his logbooks — not weather and tides, but a second record, written sideways in the margins, addressed to her. To read them in order she has to stay, and to stay she has to face the woman on the mainland who knew her father better than she did.
- **Core promise to the reader:** A quiet, devastating reckoning with a parent you only understand after they're gone — and the unbearable arithmetic of what was never said.
- **What makes it different:** The mystery is not *what happened* but *who he was*; the logbook is an unreliable narrator written by a dead man.

## §2. Target Length and Format

- **Target:** 78,000 words (acceptable range 72–86k). Standard literary novel length.
- **Structure:** Three parts, ~24 chapters. Chapter length follows dramatic function (see `book-length-and-pacing.md`), not a fixed count.
- **Format:** Ebook-first, with a print paperback. Produced via `scripts/build.sh`.

## §3. Reader Experience Design

- **First 10 pages:** Unease and competence — a woman who manages everything and feels nothing, arriving at a place that won't let her keep doing that.
- **By 25%:** Understand that the estrangement was mutual and that the logbook is going to indict her too, not just exonerate him.
- **Midpoint turn:** She finds the entry that proves her father knew she'd come back, and planned for it. The grief curdles into anger, then into something she can't name.
- **Final 20%:** The mainland woman's account collides with the logbook's. One of them is lying, and it matters less than Nora expected.
- **What they remember:** The last margin entry, and what Nora chooses not to read aloud at the end.

## §5. Character System (excerpt — one of four)

**Nora Vale — 39, protagonist.**
- **Conscious want:** Settle the estate fast and leave the island for good.
- **Unconscious need:** To be allowed to have loved him badly and still have it count.
- **Wound / ghost:** At 28 she didn't come to her mother's funeral; her father never asked why, and she never said.
- **Fatal flaw (which is also her strength):** Self-sufficiency so complete it reads as abandonment to everyone who tries to get close.
- **Arc:** From managing grief like an estate sale → to letting one thing remain unresolved on purpose.
- **Voice:** Notices logistics before feelings. Counts things. Avoids the word *Dad*.

## §6. Chapter-by-Chapter Plan (excerpt — Chapter 1)

- **Ch 1 — "Decommissioned."** Nora arrives on the last ferry of the season.
  - **Dramatic purpose:** Establish her competence-as-armor and the place's refusal to be managed.
  - **Entry → exit state:** Arrives intending to leave in three days → discovers the lamp-room logbooks and knows, against her will, that she'll stay.
  - **Key beats:** The solicitor's key doesn't fit; the neighbor who isn't surprised to see her; the first margin entry, addressed to "N."
  - **Continuity to seed:** The broken second key (pays off Ch 19); the neighbor's unexplained familiarity (pays off Ch 14).
  - **Risk:** Opening on travel logistics could stall — start at the locked door, not the ferry.

## §10. Quality Gates (per chapter)

1. Scene circuit complete (entry state ≠ exit state)?
